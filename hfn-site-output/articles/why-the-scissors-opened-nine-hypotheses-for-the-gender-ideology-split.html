Why the Scissors Opened: Nine Hypotheses for the Gender Ideology Split

15 min read ·21 February 2026
4 interactive charts
Share

A companion article published two days ago on this site — The Great Divergence: Why Young Men and Women No Longer See the Same World — laid out the statistics. A fifteen-point gap in the United States. Twenty-five in Britain. Thirty in Germany. Fifty in South Korea. Young women in every developed country have moved dramatically to the left. Young men have stayed where they were, or drifted right. The data is unambiguous.

But data tells you what. It does not tell you why. And the question of causation — not what is correlated with the divergence, but what is actually driving it — is the harder and more important question. Get the causation wrong and every proposed remedy will fail, because you will be treating symptoms rather than the disease.

What follows is an attempt to move from correlation to causation. Nine hypotheses, organised by the depth of the structural force they describe, ranked by the strength of the evidence behind them, and tested — wherever possible — against the most powerful falsification tool available: the Nordic Paradox.

The Reframing

Figure 1

The Scissors: Liberal Identification Among 18–29 Year Olds (1999–2024)

Women moved sharply left while men stayed remarkably stable — the divergence is asymmetric

Source: Source: Gallup Political Ideology surveys (1999–2024); PRRI (2024)

Before the hypotheses, a correction. The standard framing of this story — in newspapers, podcasts, and political commentary — is: “men and women are moving apart.” This framing is wrong. It implies symmetry. The movement is not symmetric.

Jordan Peterson’s 12 Rules for Life sold over five million copies, predominantly to young men, and explicitly frames progressive politics as an attack on masculine identity.

Gallup’s ideology tracking, running continuously since 1999, shows that young men’s ideological self-identification has been remarkably stable for twenty-five years. In 2000, roughly twenty-seven per cent of American men aged eighteen to twenty-nine identified as liberal. In 2024, roughly twenty-five per cent did. The change is within the margin of error. PRRI data tells a slightly different story on party identification — young men’s Democratic affiliation dropped from forty-two per cent in 2020 to thirty-two per cent in 2024, with Republican identification rising from twenty to twenty-nine per cent — but this is a party shift, not necessarily an ideological one. Men may be changing teams without changing beliefs.

Women are a different matter. In 2000, thirty per cent of young American women identified as liberal. By 2024, forty per cent did — an increase of ten percentage points in absolute terms and a tripling of the gender gap. The shift accelerated visibly after 2014, spiked in 2017, and has not reversed.

The more precise question, then, is not “why are men and women diverging?” It is two questions: “why are women moving left so rapidly?” and “why are men changing party affiliation without changing ideology?” These are different phenomena with different causes, and conflating them produces confused analysis.

The Nordic Paradox

Figure 2

The Nordic Paradox: Gender Equality vs. Gender Ideology Gap

More gender-equal countries show wider ideological gaps — equality unmasks divergence rather than closing it

Source: Source: World Economic Forum Gender Gap Index (2024); FT/Burn-Murdoch (2024); Lahtinen (2023)

The most powerful test available for any hypothesis about the gender gap is a counter-intuitive finding from Finland. The political scientist Hanna Lahtinen documented that the gender gap in political attitudes is wider in the most gender-equal societies on Earth — Sweden, Finland, Norway, Denmark — than in less egalitarian ones. This is the opposite of what most people would predict. If the gender gap were caused by oppression, discrimination, or unequal access, it should be smallest in societies that have done the most to eliminate those barriers. It is not. It is largest.

This single finding rules out an entire class of explanations. Any hypothesis that frames the gap as a product of inequality — women are more liberal because they face more discrimination, and the gap will close as equality improves — is directly falsified by the Nordic data. The gap is a feature of equality, not a legacy of inequality. The hypotheses that survive this test are the ones worth taking seriously.

Tier 1: The Foundations

These are the slow-moving structural forces that created the conditions for divergence over decades. They explain direction and magnitude, though not timing.

Hypothesis 1: The Contraception Long Arc

Claudia Goldin, who received the Nobel Prize in Economics in 2023, demonstrated that the approval of the combined oral contraceptive pill in 1960 fundamentally altered women’s economic horizons. Before the pill, women could not reliably invest in long professional education — law, medicine, finance, graduate study — because pregnancy could derail any career plan before the investment paid off. After the pill, they could. For the first time in history, women could plan a professional life with the same time horizon as men.

The downstream chain is long but legible: contraception enabled career investment, which enabled economic independence, which enabled later marriage, which weakened social interdependence with men, which reduced the need to compromise on political values with a partner, which allowed underlying value differences to be expressed politically. The generation of economically independent Boomer and Gen X mothers raised Gen Z daughters with the expectation that financial dependence on a man was neither necessary nor desirable. What we are measuring now is not a sudden change in values. It is the culmination of a sixty-year structural shift in women’s relationship to the economy.

This hypothesis explains the direction of the divergence — women moving left — but not the timing of its acceleration. The pill has been available since 1960. The divergence accelerated around 2012. Something else must account for the acceleration.

Hypothesis 2: The Education Inversion

Figure 3

The Education Engine: Women's Degree Attainment and the Ideology Gap

As the education gap widened, the ideology gap followed — education explains roughly half the divergence

Source: Source: OECD Education at a Glance (2024); Gallup; EdWorkingPapers (2025)

Women now earn approximately sixty per cent of all bachelor’s degrees in the United States. In the European Union, forty-six per cent of women aged twenty-five to thirty-four hold tertiary degrees, compared with thirty-five per cent of men — a gap that was effectively zero in 1990 and has doubled since 2002. In Canada, seventy-six per cent of young women hold degrees versus fifty-eight per cent of young men. Twenty-eight per cent of boys across OECD countries fail basic reading benchmarks, compared with eighteen per cent of girls (Economist, 2024).

This matters for political attitudes because college attendance has a causal — not merely correlational — effect on political liberalisation. A 2023 study using propensity-score weighting (a method designed to isolate causation from self-selection) found that attending college itself shifts political orientation leftward, independent of the characteristics that predispose certain people to attend college in the first place (Stolzenberg and Reardon, 2023). The effect is strongest in the humanities and social sciences — fields that have skewed heavily female. A British cohort study confirmed this as a “learning effect,” not mere selection: people who study arts and social sciences emerge more socially liberal than people with similar pre-university characteristics who studied other subjects.

An EdWorkingPapers analysis (2025) estimated that differences in educational attainment explain approximately fifty per cent of the gender gap in voting behaviour. This is the single largest identified driver — half the gap, from one variable. If men’s educational levels matched women’s, the gap would shrink substantially.

The education hypothesis also explains why the gap first appeared among the highly educated in the 1960s and 1970s — when women began entering universities in significant numbers — but did not appear among the less educated until the 1980s, when party polarisation became visible enough for less-educated voters to sort themselves ideologically.

Hypothesis 3: Economic Displacement and Status Loss

American manufacturing employment peaked at 19.6 million in 1979. By 2010, it had fallen to 11.5 million. These were disproportionately male jobs — well-paid, stable, and central to working-class masculine identity. Men without a high school diploma earned four dollars per hour more than women with a high school diploma in 1973. By 2016, they earned $1.20 per hour less — a relative decline of $5.20 per hour over four decades.

Labour force participation for high-school-educated men aged twenty-five to thirty-four dropped from ninety-eight per cent to eighty-seven per cent between 1970 and 2020. David Autor, David Dorn, and Gordon Hanson at MIT documented that the communities hit hardest by manufacturing decline showed the sharpest rightward political shifts. In Austria, a peer-reviewed study found that the decline of manufacturing employment explained approximately one-third of the observed increase in far-right vote share between 1995 and 2019, with trade competition and automation contributing roughly equally.

Noah Zucker’s research on “breadwinner backlash” identifies the mechanism: when men lose their economic role as primary earner, they are drawn toward political movements that promise to restore traditional gender hierarchies — movements that are overwhelmingly on the right. The MIT team documented the downstream chain in affected communities: economic decline leads to reduced male “marriageability,” which leads to family breakdown, which leads to social isolation, which increases susceptibility to populist political messaging.

This hypothesis is strong for explaining the rightward party shift among non-college men. It is weaker for college-educated men, who are also drifting right at a modest rate despite generally favourable economic outcomes.

Tier 2: The Amplifiers

These forces did not create the divergence. They accelerated it — massively — beginning around 2012–2015.

Hypothesis 4: Algorithmic Divergence

Figure 4

The Algorithm Machine: How TikTok Sorts Content by Gender

Male-presenting accounts saw misogynistic content rise from 13% to 56% of recommendations in just five days

Source: Source: UCL/Dublin City University quasi-experimental study (2024)

A quasi-experimental study by researchers at University College London and Dublin City University (2024) created TikTok accounts designed to simulate teenage boys. The accounts were shown mild gender-normative content — fitness, self-improvement, finance. Within five days, the proportion of recommended content that was misogynistic rose from thirteen per cent to fifty-six per cent. The algorithm did not require the user to seek out extreme content. It delivered it as a byproduct of consuming adjacent material — optimising, as all engagement algorithms do, for the content most likely to keep the user scrolling.

A Princeton study analysed over thirty thousand YouTube videos and documented the pipeline: anti-SJW commentary leads to manosphere content, which leads to overtly political far-right material. The pipeline operates through recommendation algorithms that progressively escalate the intensity of content along a predictable ideological trajectory.

The timing is precise. The divergence in political attitudes accelerated sharply between 2012 and 2013 across multiple English-speaking countries simultaneously — the period when smartphone penetration among teenagers crossed the fifty per cent threshold. Jonathan Haidt and Jean Twenge have both identified this as the inflection point: not just for the gender gap, but for adolescent mental health, social behaviour, and political identity formation.

The result is that men and women under thirty are consuming different media, absorbing different narratives, and forming different pictures of reality. This is not a metaphor. It is a documented, quantified divergence in information environments created by algorithms that optimise for engagement rather than truth.

One important caveat: Norway has similar social media penetration but does not show a significant gender gap, suggesting that algorithmic divergence is a necessary but not sufficient condition. It requires pre-existing structural vulnerability — economic displacement, educational divergence — to operate on.

Hypothesis 5: The Influencer Pipeline

The algorithms create the information environment. The influencers provide the ideology that fills it.

Jordan Peterson’s 12 Rules for Life sold over five million copies, predominantly to young men, and explicitly frames progressive politics as an attack on masculine identity. Andrew Tate, before his arrest, accumulated 11.6 billion views on TikTok. A 2024 analysis published in Sage Journals examined 150,000 words of Tate’s content and found systematic “ideological scaffolding” — self-help advice layered with anti-feminist politics, entrepreneurial aspiration mixed with misogynistic worldview, financial guidance that segues into conspiracy theory. The architecture is deliberate: the gateway is self-improvement, the destination is political radicalisation.

The Young Men Research Initiative (2024) found that fifty per cent of young men surveyed held a favourable view of Tate. In the United Kingdom, forty-five per cent of males aged sixteen to twenty-four viewed him positively. In Australia, twenty-five per cent of males aged twelve to eighteen considered him a role model.

The influencer pipeline is distinct from algorithmic divergence — it is the content that fills the algorithmically created void. It explains how economic grievance and social isolation are converted into a coherent political identity. Without the influencer pipeline, economic displacement might produce apathy rather than right-wing mobilisation. With it, grievance finds a narrative, and the narrative has a political address.

Tier 3: The Crystallisation

Hypothesis 6: Asymmetric Political Mobilisation

A series of political events since 2016 served as crystallisation moments for young women — converting diffuse progressive sympathy into active, durable political identity. Men experienced no equivalent mobilising event on the left.

The Women’s March of January 2017 was the largest single-day protest in American history. The #MeToo movement, which erupted in October 2017, was not merely a news story but a mass collective political experience. Research published in Political Research Quarterly found that MeToo significantly increased political engagement, particularly among women who had personally experienced workplace harassment and among Democratic-identifying women.

The Dobbs decision of June 2022 — overturning Roe v. Wade — was the most potent gender-specific mobilising event in recent American political history. The American Survey Center reported that abortion became the number-one issue for young women after Dobbs, outranking inflation, jobs, and healthcare. Post-Dobbs, Democrats gained in virtually every election where reproductive rights were on the ballot — and the gender gap in those elections was dramatically larger than in pre-Dobbs contests. Sixty per cent of single women reported believing that women’s lives would become worse under Republican governance.

The timing of young women’s leftward shift tracks these events precisely: acceleration after 2016, spike in 2017–2018, intensification after 2022. But this hypothesis has a critical limitation: the gender gap is equally large in Germany, South Korea, and the United Kingdom, none of which experienced these specific American events. Trump, MeToo, and Dobbs were amplifiers of an existing trend, not root causes.

Tier 4: The Feedback Loops

Hypothesis 7: The Collapse of Cross-Gender Relationships

Marriage historically functioned as a political moderating mechanism — couples negotiated worldviews across gender lines over decades, producing convergence. Research consistently shows that married couples’ political views converge over time. That mechanism is failing.

Marriage rates among eighteen-to-twenty-nine-year-olds are at historic lows across every developed country. Two-thirds of singles now report that they would reject a potential partner who does not share their politics. Only thirty-five per cent of unmarried women view dating apps as safe — down from fifty-three per cent in 2019. Fifty-five per cent of singles feel pessimistic about finding a long-term partner.

South Korean data illustrates the extreme: men in their twenties rate women aged twenty to thirty at 37.4 out of 100 on favourability scales; women reciprocate at 38.6 out of 100. The sexes in their most fertile decade actively dislike each other — and that dislike has mapped directly onto voting behaviour, with the gender gap in the 2025 election reaching thirty-eight percentage points.

The feedback loop is clear: political divergence makes cross-gender relationships harder to form. Fewer relationships mean less moderating contact. Less moderating contact deepens the divergence. Deeper divergence makes relationships still harder to form. The cycle is self-reinforcing, and no country that has entered it has yet found the exit.

Hypothesis 8: Zero-Sum Framing and the Backlash Loop

Forty per cent of men under fifty who supported Trump in 2024 agreed that women’s gains in society had come at the expense of men — more than double the share among Biden-supporting men of the same age. Young men are twice as likely as men over sixty-five to believe that women “have it easier” in today’s society — a statistic that should give pause, since it suggests this is a learned, generationally specific belief rather than a timeless male grievance.

The mechanism is discursive. Terms that have entered mainstream progressive vocabulary since approximately 2015 — “male privilege,” “toxic masculinity,” “patriarchy,” “check your privilege” — frame male identity as a structural problem to be solved rather than a constituency to be persuaded. For men who do not personally feel privileged — who are struggling economically, educationally, or socially — the message that they are the beneficiaries of systemic advantage is not just wrong in their experience. It is alienating. And alienated constituencies do not reform. They defect.

Each side’s movement reinforces the other. More assertive feminist politics produces more defensive male backlash, which produces more assertive feminist politics, in a ratchet that tightens with each turn. The Ipsos Equalities Index (2025), surveying thirty countries, found that fifty-one per cent of respondents perceive tension between men and women — fifty-nine per cent among Gen Z, compared with forty per cent among Baby Boomers. The youngest generation feels the tension most acutely because they are living inside the ratchet.

Tier 5: The Deep Structure

Hypothesis 9: The Moral Foundations Asymmetry

Jonathan Haidt’s Moral Foundations Theory proposes that human moral reasoning operates across six foundations: Care/Harm, Fairness/Cheating, Loyalty/Betrayal, Authority/Subversion, Sanctity/Degradation, and Liberty/Oppression. Cross-cultural research consistently shows that women weight Care/Harm and Fairness more heavily, while men weight Loyalty, Authority, and Sanctity more heavily.

Modern progressive politics operates primarily on the Care/Harm and Fairness foundations — social justice, welfare, equality, protection of vulnerable groups. Modern conservative politics operates more heavily on Loyalty, Authority, and Sanctity — national identity, institutional order, tradition, in-group cohesion. If the moral foundations are even partially innate (which the cross-cultural consistency suggests), then progressive politics is structurally more resonant with women’s moral intuitions, and conservative politics with men’s.

For most of human history, this predisposition was suppressed. Women’s economic dependence on men — as breadwinners, as property owners, as legal agents — forced political convergence within households. A woman whose survival depended on her husband could not afford to vote against his interests, regardless of her moral intuitions. As economic independence removed this constraint — through the pill, through education, through the service economy, through welfare-state provisions — the underlying moral divergence was free to express itself politically.

This hypothesis explains the Nordic Paradox. The societies with the highest female economic independence are the ones where the constraint has been most thoroughly removed — and therefore where the underlying values divergence is most fully expressed. The gap is not a sign of inequality’s persistence. It is a sign of its successful removal.

The weakness of this hypothesis is that it risks essentialising gender differences — treating them as biological destiny rather than a complex interaction of biology and culture. But the cross-cultural data is difficult to dismiss. The foundations show consistent gender patterns across dozens of societies with vastly different cultural contexts. Something beyond culture is at work.

The Integrated Model

No single hypothesis is sufficient. The most defensible causal account treats them as layered and interactive.

Layer zero is the contraceptive revolution — the sixty-year structural shift that enabled everything else by giving women economic independence for the first time in human history.

Layer one is the education inversion and male economic displacement — two slow-moving forces that, from the 1970s onward, sent men and women on diverging life trajectories that produced diverging political receptivity.

Layer two is algorithmic social media and the influencer pipeline — the amplification machinery that, from 2012 onward, took pre-existing tendencies and sorted them into gender-segregated information realities at unprecedented speed and scale.

Layer three is political shock mobilisation — the specific events (Trump, MeToo, Dobbs) that converted latent progressive identity in women into active, durable political commitment, with no equivalent mobilisation on the male left.

Layer four is the feedback loops — the collapse of cross-gender relationships and the zero-sum backlash ratchet that compound the divergence and prevent it from self-correcting.

Layer five is the moral foundations asymmetry — the deepest structural reality, which the other layers have progressively unmasked.

The model explains why the gap appeared when it did (education and economic forces building since the 1970s), why it accelerated when it did (smartphone adoption and algorithmic sorting from 2012), why it spiked when it did (political shocks from 2016–2022), why it is self-reinforcing (feedback loops), and why it is widest in the most equal societies (the Nordic Paradox, explained by moral foundations theory).

What the Data Cannot Yet Prove

Three questions remain genuinely open.

First, whether young men are actually becoming more conservative or merely switching party labels. The PRRI data shows ideological stability but partisan movement. If men are adopting Republican identity as a tribal affiliation — a way to signal opposition to progressive cultural messaging — without shifting their underlying policy views, the divergence is shallower than it appears and potentially more reversible.

Second, which country’s mechanism is most generalisable. South Korea’s fifty-point gap predates Tate, Peterson, and TikTok. It was triggered by an explicit feminist-versus-anti-feminist political war during the Moon presidency, combined with compulsory male military service and brutal labour-market competition. The structural hypotheses — education, economics, moral foundations — travel across borders. The amplification hypotheses may be more culturally specific.

Third, whether this is a permanent realignment or a cohort effect that will moderate as this generation ages, forms partnerships, and enters the workforce. Previous political gender gaps — the conservative tendency among women after suffrage, for instance — did eventually close. But they did not close quickly, and the mechanisms driving today’s gap (algorithmic sorting, relationship collapse, economic restructuring) show no sign of reversing on their own.

South Korea is the canary in the coal mine. A thirty-eight-point gender gap. A fertility rate of 0.72. A generation of men and women who rate each other below forty out of a hundred. The scissors are open widest there. The rest of the developed world is tracking the same trajectory, at varying speeds. The question for every society watching Seoul is not whether the scissors are opening. It is whether anyone has a plan to close them.
