Why God Needs The Government: Multiculturalism Vs Monotheism

HFN looks at the worship of Aton, the first monotheistic religion over 3,350 years ago. We examine how changes in European attitudes towards Christianity today may make Islam more rigid in the future. Multiculturalism and strict monotheism are incompatible.

Most people will have heard of Tutankhamon, the young Egyptian Pharaoh whose tomb was rediscovered, intact, in 1922. He was not always called Tutankh amon , however. His original name was Tutankh aton and he succeeded his less famous, but significantly more important, father-in-law Akhen aton, who died in 1336 BC. Both Pharaohs had part of their name in common - Aton .

Akhen aton (“Splendour of Aton ”) had been born Amon hotep (“Beloved of Amon ”) and at some point in his life he stopped being a polytheist and started to exclusively worship Aton, a new god. Curiously for the time, Aton was a god that had no physical form at all. He was just to be imagined and no depictions of Aton were allowed – except for a “sun disk”, which is what Aton means.

Akhen aton - who was married to the beautiful Nefertiti – forcibly converted the entire country of Egypt into becoming worshipers of Aton, at the expense of all of the other traditional gods. As Pharaoh, and in charge of all of the wealth of the state, Akhen aton had the power and authority to close rival shrines and temples have the names of other gods removed from monuments. He abandoned the ancient Egyptian capital of Thebes and built a new capital on the banks of the Nile. What was unique about Akhen aton was that he did not just believe that there was one chief god but he also believed that there was only one god and that all other gods were false. Aton was thus the world’s first monotheistic god, long before the Jews dedicated themselves to their one god, Yahweh.

It was no easy task to permanently rid the country of all of the other gods. After Akhenaton died in 1336 BCE the capital moved back to Thebes. The god Aton was rejected by Egypt and the ruler Akhen aton’s name was in turn chiseled from stone inscriptions on monuments that he had erected. Akhenaton’s name was also removed from most of the official records of Egyptian Pharaohs; it is amazing that we know anything about him at all. Freed from his father-in-law’s influence, Tutankh aton changed his name to the more familiar Tutankh amon.

Strict monotheism required the backing of the state to thrive. Without that support it died. The natural religious order for ancient Egypt was polytheism.

The fundamental difference between monotheism and polytheism is that polytheists can happily co-exist with other religious groups. In fact they thrive on picking and choosing the best elements of other gods, no matter where they come from. The Romans and Greeks were masters at this and frequently used double barreled names to describe their gods, connecting a more familiar god with a more foreign god. This preference encourages multiculturalism and the exchange of goods and ideas.

Monotheism, however, requires the denial of the existence of other gods. If your god is the one and only true god, it follows that all other gods must be false. For others to believe in those false gods is an affront to your one and only god. This affront must be excised, and to do this effectively your faith must dominate completely.

Once monotheism, in the form of Judaism, followed by Christianity got underway, however, there came to be a realisation that it was not enough to have one true god. It was necessary to have one way of worshipping and interpreting that one true god. All other ways threatened your existence. These schisms explain the external conflicts between the Abrahamic religions of Judaism, Christianity and Islam, and the internal conflicts between Christian sects and Shia and Sunni Islam. The suppression of science in the medieval world resulted as new ideas came into conflict with long held religious belief. New scientific ideas, like Galileo’s helio-centric solar system, were a threat to monotheistic dominance.

The preeminence followed by the decline of Monotheism also partially explains the simultaneous rise in multiculturalism in Europe and the fall in the number of regular Christian churchgoers in the modern era. As fewer Europeans are Christian believers in general, and specific Christian sect members in particular, there is less of a need for Europeans to demand that all other people follow their faith and sect. Not only is it widely accepted that people might be agnostic or atheist (which was originally a derogatory term for Christians who denied the existence of the traditional pantheon of Roman gods) but also that the existence of other gods can be tolerated as well.

This is one of the reasons that modern Europe is so effective from a multicultural perspective. Spain, France, Italy, Germany, Britain, Romania, Sweden etc all have different languages, cultures and religions – albeit variations on Christianity. Within their borders Europeans and non-European peoples are accepted. Freedom of religion is enshrined in European law. A requirement for all people living in Europe to be faithful to one Christian God of one Christian sect would make pan-European co-operation significantly harder.

This tolerance is in sharp contrast to many Muslim countries. States which forbid open worship of religions other than Islam, countries where Muslims kill fellow Muslims due to doctrinal differences. Conversion away from Islam (apostasy) is a capital offence, while becoming a Muslim is automatic at birth. Multi-culturalism is effectively impossible in those countries.

As more non-Europeans of Islamic heritage live in the European Union it could have the effect of making Islam more tolerant in Islamic countries. Migrants will have seen that it is possible to worship their own god alongside non-believers and to live in harmony.

Equally, it could be seen as a cautionary tale. Once people are allowed to stop believing in a particular religion or to abandon their faith for another faith, or even to drop religion entirely, they may. To prevent this abandonment a logical reaction is for religious zealots to fight harder to make a particular religion more powerful and more inclined to subjugate and brainwash its worshippers.

Monotheism is hard to maintain without the firm backing of the state.
